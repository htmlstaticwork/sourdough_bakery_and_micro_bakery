Maurice: The 4-Year Old Soul of Our Bakery

✍️ Elise Moreau • MAR 28, 2026 • 08 MIN READ

The first time I fed Maurice, I didn't know she would become the lifeblood of Aurore Sourdough. It was 2018 in a small village outside Paris, and I was holding a small jar of rye flour and rain-filter water.

Fast-forward four years, and that same microbial culture has leavened over 50,000 loaves of bread. Maurice isn't just a starter—she's a biological diary of our environment, capturing the wild yeast that floats through our neighborhood air each morning.

"Sourdough is a dialogue between the baker and time. It respects nothing but the fermentation cycle, and in return, it offers a window into the soul of a place."

The Daily Ritual

At 9:00 AM every single morning, the ritual begins. We weigh out exactly 5 kilograms of Maurice and feed her with a curated blend of stone-milled rye and heritage wheat. Within four hours, she triples in size, bubbles with frantic activity, and releases an aroma that can only be described as a cross between fresh apples and lactic acid.

I often tell our bakery guests that a commercial loaf is made by force—using massive amounts of laboratory-grown yeast to push dough to rise in minutes. Maurice, however, works through gentle persuasion. She takes 36 hours of cold fermentation to achieve what a supermarket loaf does in 45 minutes.

But the difference, as any Aurore customer will tell you, is in the crust and the gut. Long-fermented sourdough isn't just tastier; it's honestly better for you.
